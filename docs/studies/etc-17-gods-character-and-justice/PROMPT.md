# Bible Study: God's Character and Justice — Final Fate of the Wicked

## Question

What does Scripture reveal about God's character — his love, justice, holiness, mercy, and wrath — and how does that character bear on the question of the final fate of the wicked? Is eternal conscious torment (ECT) or annihilation/destruction more consistent with who God is revealed to be? What biblical passages speak to God's purposes in judgment?

### Key Investigation Areas

Derived from tool discoveries:

1. **God's justice — proportionality, retribution, purposes of judgment.** Nave's "GOD, JUDGE, AND HIS JUSTICE" supplies 80+ verse references spanning all testaments. Key texts: Gen 18:25 ("Shall not the Judge of all the earth do right?"); EZK 18:25-32 (God's justice questioned and defended — specifically about proportional individual judgment); ROM 2:2-16 (judgment according to deeds); 2TH 1:4-6 ("righteous judgment" includes that it be "righteous"). The investigation must ask: is perpetual ongoing torment proportional justice, or does God's own declared standard of justice (which emphasizes proportionality: Luk 12:47-48) point toward a measured, finite result?

2. **God's love and mercy — how they relate to final judgment.** Nave's entries for GOD, LOVE OF and GOD, MERCY OF are among the largest in the Bible. HOS 11:8-9 is critical: "How shall I give thee up, Ephraim?... mine heart is turned within me, my repentings are kindled together. I will not execute the fierceness of mine anger... for I am God, and not man; the Holy One in the midst of thee." God's love does not prevent judgment (he does judge) but his character as "not man" is invoked AGAINST perpetual fierce anger. Also critical: EZK 18:23,32 and 33:11 — God explicitly states he takes "no pleasure in the death of the wicked." Investigation must assess: is ECT consistent with these statements, or does annihilation/destruction better fit the declaration that God does not delight in the destruction of the wicked?

3. **God's holiness and wrath — what they demand.** HAB 1:12-13 states God is "of purer eyes than to behold evil" — holiness demands that sin be addressed. NAM 1:2-6 describes God's character as a "jealous God and avenging." ISA 57:15-16 is critical: God is "the high and lofty One that inhabiteth eternity, whose name is Holy" — yet immediately: "I will not contend for ever, neither will I be always wroth." The investigation must examine whether God's holiness demands ongoing eternal conscious torment specifically, or whether it demands complete and final elimination of wickedness.

4. **Biblical purposes of judgment — vindication, correction, demonstration of righteousness.** Judgment in Scripture serves multiple purposes: (a) vindicating God's righteousness (PSA 9:4; ROM 3:4-6); (b) demonstrating justice before all creation (REV 15:3; 16:5-7; 19:2 — "righteous are thy judgments"); (c) removing evil from God's renewed creation (REV 21:8; 21:27 — "there shall in no wise enter into it any thing that defileth"). The investigation must ask whether ongoing eternal conscious torment serves the stated purposes of judgment better than annihilation does, particularly whether perpetual tormenting of creatures serves the purpose of vindicating God's righteousness or whether it undermines the picture of God as perfectly just and merciful.

5. **Whether ECT or annihilation better fits the accumulated biblical portrait of God.** This is the synthesizing question. The investigation draws on the character attributes discovered above alongside the prior series evidence (destruction vocabulary, immortality evidence, strongest ECT case assessment) to evaluate which doctrine more consistently represents the God described in Scripture. Key texts identified from both the Nave's searches and the series context: ISA 61:8 ("I the LORD love judgment"); PSA 145:17 ("The LORD is righteous in all his ways, and holy in all his works"); REV 16:5-7 ("Thou art righteous, O Lord... even so, Lord God Almighty, true and righteous are thy judgments"); 2SA 14:14 ("God doth not take away life; but deviseth means, that his banished be not expelled from him") — God's character as not delighting in death is a key datum.
