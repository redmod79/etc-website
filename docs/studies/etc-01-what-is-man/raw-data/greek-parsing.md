καὶ μὴ φοβεῖσθε ἀπὸ τῶν ἀποκτεννόντων τὸ σῶμα, τὴν δὲ ψυχὴν μὴ δυναμένων ἀποκτεῖναι· φοβεῖσθε δὲ μᾶλλον τὸν δυνάμενον καὶ ψυχὴν καὶ σῶμα ἀπολέσαι ἐν γεέννῃ.

**Key grammatical analysis:**
1. The contrast: "those who kill the body but are not able to kill the soul" vs. "the one who is able to destroy BOTH soul AND body in Gehenna."
2. ἀπολέσαι (apolesai) — aorist active infinitive of ἀπόλλυμι (to destroy/ruin). This is the same word used for perishing in Jn 3:16 ("should not perish"). The soul CAN be destroyed by God.
3. Both ψυχή and σῶμα are accusative — both are objects of ἀπολέσαι (destroy). The parallelism is καὶ ψυχὴν καὶ σῶμα — "both soul AND body."
4. The verse does NOT say the soul is inherently indestructible; on the contrary, God CAN destroy it.

**Key grammatical analysis:**
1. ὁ μόνος ἔχων ἀθανασίαν — "the only one HAVING immortality." μόνος (monos) = alone, only, unique. This is a strong exclusionary term: God is THE ONLY BEING who possesses immortality as an inherent quality.
2. ἔχων (ekhon) — present active participle — habitual possession. God perpetually, inherently has immortality.
3. The verse is in the context of doxology about God — it is a descriptive attribute, not a statement about what humans may acquire.
4. Contrast with 1 Cor 15:53-54 where mortals must "put on" (ἐνδύω, to clothe oneself) immortality — it is not inherent.

Ἐγένετο ὁ πρῶτος ἄνθρωπος Ἀδὰμ εἰς ψυχὴν ζῶσαν· ὁ ἔσχατος Ἀδὰμ εἰς πνεῦμα ζωοποιοῦν.

|-----------------|------------|---------|--------------------------------------|

| ὁ ἔσχατος Ἀδὰμ  | ἔσχατος    | G2078   | A-NSM-S (the last)                   |
| εἰς πνεῦμα      | πνεῦμα     | G4151   | N-ASN (spirit)                       |

**Key grammatical analysis:**
1. Ἐγένετο...εἰς ψυχὴν ζῶσαν — "became into a living soul." Paul is CITING Gen 2:7 from the LXX: ἐγένετο ὁ ἄνθρωπος εἰς ψυχὴν ζῶσαν. The εἰς (into) + accusative indicates transformation/result — Adam BECAME a living soul.
2. ζῶσαν (zōsan) — present active participle of ζάω (to live) — "living." The soul is not simply an inner substance; it is the living-being state.
3. The contrast: first Adam = ψυχή (psyche, natural life/soul); last Adam = πνεῦμα ζωοποιοῦν (life-giving spirit). The ψυχικός body (natural) contrasted with πνευματικός body (spiritual) in v.44.
4. This verse is Paul's NT commentary on Gen 2:7 — confirming that man "became" (not "received") a living soul.

3. τὸ φθαρτόν = "the perishable one/thing"; τὸ θνητόν = "the mortal one/thing." These describe the current state of all humans — perishable and mortal.
4. The future putting-on of immortality contrasts with 1 Tim 6:16 (only God currently HAS immortality inherently).

| τηρηθείη    | τηρέω     | G5083   | V-APO-3S Aor Pass Opt (be kept)  |

**Key grammatical analysis:**
1. Three terms: πνεῦμα (spirit), ψυχή (soul), σῶμα (body) — all nominative, subject of τηρηθείη.
2. ὁλόκληρον (holoklēron) = "whole, complete" — the prayer is for the WHOLE person to be preserved. The three terms are comprehensive — "the entirety of you — spirit and soul and body."
3. The passage does NOT argue that spirit and soul are ontologically distinct substances. It is a rhetorical intensification (like "heart, soul, mind, and strength" in Mark 12:30) emphasizing wholeness.
4. τηρηθείη — aorist passive optative — a prayer/wish that God would preserve them.

2. This is NOT claiming that soul and spirit are already separated substances — rather, the word of God penetrates so deeply that it can distinguish between them, like a surgeon's knife distinguishing joints from marrow.
3. The parallel structure: [soul and spirit] :: [joints and marrow] — both pairs are compared to the depth of penetration, not to ontological distinctness.
4. κριτικὸς (kritikos) — "discerning/judging" — the word discerns the THOUGHTS and INTENTS of the heart. The point is the word's penetrating power, not a teaching about the constitution of humanity.

## James 2:26 — Greek Parsing

ὥσπερ γὰρ τὸ σῶμα χωρὶς πνεύματος νεκρόν ἐστιν...

| Word        | Lemma    | Strong  | Parsing                     |
|-------------|----------|---------|-----------------------------|
| τὸ σῶμα     | σῶμα     | G4983   | N-NSN (the body)            |
| χωρὶς       | χωρίς    | G5565   | ADV (apart from, without)   |
| πνεύματος   | πνεῦμα   | G4151   | N-GSN Gen Sg N (of spirit)  |
| νεκρόν      | νεκρός   | G3498   | A-NSN (dead)                |
| ἐστιν       | εἰμί     | G1510   | V-PAI-3S (is)               |

**Key grammatical analysis:**
1. σῶμα χωρὶς πνεύματος — "the body apart from spirit" = dead. The spirit is what animates the body.
2. This is an analogy: as body without spirit = dead body (corpse), so faith without works = dead faith.
3. The use of πνεύματος here is debated — it could refer to the human spirit (life-force) or the Holy Spirit. Given the analogy to physical death, the natural reading is the spirit that animates physical life — consistent with Job 27:3; Jas 2:26 echoes Gen 2:7 reversed: remove the breath/spirit and the body dies.

## Revelation 16:3 — Greek Parsing

...καὶ πᾶσα ψυχὴ ζωῆς ἀπέθανεν, τὰ ἐν τῇ θαλάσσῃ.

| Word        | Lemma         | Strong  | Parsing                     |
|-------------|---------------|---------|-----------------------------|
| πᾶσα        | πᾶς           | G3956   | A-NSF (all/every)           |
| ψυχὴ        | ψυχή          | G5590   | N-NSF Nom Sg F (soul)       |
| ζωῆς        | ζωή           | G2222   | N-GSF Gen Sg F (of life)    |
| ἀπέθανεν    | ἀποθνῄσκω     | G599    | V-2AAI-3S Aor Act Ind (died)|

**Key grammatical analysis:**
1. πᾶσα ψυχὴ ζωῆς ἀπέθανεν — "every living soul died." ψυχὴ ζωῆς is the Greek rendering of the Hebrew נֶפֶשׁ חַיָּה (nephesh chayyah, living soul).
2. ἀπέθανεν — "died" — the aorist indicative, straightforward statement of death. The SOUL died. This is unambiguous: the soul is mortal, not inherently immortal.
3. This verse applies to the sea creatures — cf. Gen 1:20-21 where sea creatures are also called nephesh chayyah. The same phrase used for animals in Genesis is used here to describe their death.
