## Section 3: "How Long" Lament Pattern

### Psalm 13:1-6

> **13:1** To the chief Musician, A Psalm of David. How long wilt thou forget me, O LORD? for ever? how long wilt thou hide thy face from me?
>
> **13:2** How long shall I take counsel in my soul, [having] sorrow in my heart daily? how long shall mine enemy be exalted over me?
>
> **13:3** Consider [and] hear me, O LORD my God: lighten mine eyes, lest I sleep the [sleep of] death;
>
> **13:4** Lest mine enemy say, I have prevailed against him; [and] those that trouble me rejoice when I am moved.
>
> **13:5** But I have trusted in thy mercy; my heart shall rejoice in thy salvation.
>
> **13:6** I will sing unto the LORD, because he hath dealt bountifully with me.

**Notes:** The "how long" formula in Psalm 13 is a lament formula spoken by a living person. The fourfold "how long" (vv.1-2) expresses temporal anguish at divine silence, not an assertion of the speaker's metaphysical state. Rev 6:10 uses the same formula (heōs pote — "until when") — a standard lament/imprecatory invocation, not a proof of the speaker's consciousness.

## Section 4: Blood-Crying-from-Ground / Personification (Gen 4; Job 16; Isa 26; Heb 11:4; 12:24)

### Genesis 4:8-11

> **4:8** And Cain talked with Abel his brother: and it came to pass, when they were in the field, that Cain rose up against Abel his brother, and slew him.
>
> **4:9** And the LORD said unto Cain, Where [is] Abel thy brother? And he said, I know not: [Am] I my brother's keeper?
>
> **4:10** And he said, What hast thou done? the voice of thy brother's blood crieth unto me from the ground.
>
> **4:11** And now [art] thou cursed from the earth, which hath opened her mouth to receive thy brother's blood from thy hand;

**Critical notes:**
- Abel is dead (v.8: "slew him"). Abel is not conscious. Yet "the voice of thy brother's blood crieth unto me from the ground" (v.10).
- The blood that cries is not Abel as a conscious entity — Abel is dead, lying in the field. The blood represents the injustice of murder crying out for divine response.
- This is the foundational OT personification pattern: blood = life, and shed blood "cries" in the sense of calling for justice.
- The Hebrew: qol demey akhikhā tso'aqim — "voice of bloods of your brother are crying." The plural "bloods" (demey) is a Hebrew idiom for violent blood-shedding, often used in contexts of murder calling for justice (Gen 9:5; Num 35:33; 2 Sam 16:8).
- v.11: "the earth which hath opened her mouth to receive thy brother's blood" — the earth is also personified as an entity that "receives" blood. Personification is the literary frame throughout.

---

### Job 16:18

> **16:18** O earth, cover not thou my blood, and let my cry have no place.

**Notes:** Job (alive) calls on the earth not to cover his blood — appealing to the same "blood-crying" tradition as Gen 4:10. If Job were killed unjustly, his blood should cry out. This is a legal invocation of the blood-cry tradition, made by a living person anticipating unjust death. The tradition itself proves that "blood crying" is a juridical/literary convention, not a metaphysical claim about the state of the dead.

---

### Isaiah 26:21

> **26:21** For, behold, the LORD cometh out of his place to punish the inhabitants of the earth for their iniquity: the earth also shall disclose her blood, and shall no more cover her slain.

**Notes:** At the final judgment, "the earth shall disclose her blood" — the shed blood of the innocent, long held in the earth, will be "disclosed" as evidence for divine judgment. This is the eschatological fulfillment of the blood-cry pattern. The "crying" blood is not a claim about conscious existence; it is a claim about divine justice that will be executed at the judgment.

---

### Hebrews 11:4

> **11:4** By faith Abel offered unto God a more excellent sacrifice than Cain, by which he obtained witness that he was righteous, God testifying of his gifts: and by it he being dead yet speaketh.

**Greek analysis:**
- ἀποθανὼν (apothanōn): Aor Act Ptcp Nom Sg M — "having died" / "being dead" — Abel is definitively described as dead
- ἔτι λαλεῖ (eti lalei): Pres Act Ind Sg 3rd — "yet speaks" / "still speaks" — present tense, continuous action
- The subject of "speaks" is the faith/sacrifice (dia autēs — "by it") — Abel speaks through his sacrifice, not through conscious awareness
- The author does NOT say Abel is consciously experiencing anything; he says Abel's faith "speaks" through the record of his sacrifice — the same literary personification as Gen 4:10

---

### Hebrews 12:24

> **12:24** And to Jesus the mediator of the new covenant, and to the blood of sprinkling, that speaketh better things than [that of] Abel.

**Greek analysis:**
- λαλοῦντι (lalounti): Pres Act Ptcp Dat Sg M — "speaking" — the participle modifies "blood of sprinkling" (aimati rantismou)
- The blood of Jesus "speaks better things than Abel's [blood]" — this is an explicit parallel: both Abel's blood (Gen 4:10) and Christ's blood "speak." Both are dead — both bloods are inert liquid. The "speaking" is figurative throughout: the blood testifies to the terms of covenant and the demand for justice.
- This confirms that "speaking/crying blood" is a consistent NT literary convention, not a claim about conscious entities.

## Section 5: Altar Blood in Levitical Law (Lev 4:7; 17:11; Ex 29:12)

### Leviticus 4:7

> **4:7** And the priest shall put [some] of the blood upon the horns of the altar of sweet incense before the LORD, which [is] in the tabernacle of the congregation; and shall pour all the blood of the bullock at the bottom of the altar of the burnt offering, which [is at] the door of the tabernacle of the congregation.

**Notes:** The blood of the sin offering is poured at the "bottom" (yesod — foundation/base) of the altar. "Under" the altar = at its base. In Rev 6:9, the souls are "under" (hypokatō) the altar — the same positional language as the Levitical blood poured at the altar's base. The sacrificial life-blood (nephesh) that was poured at the altar's foundation is the typological forerunner of the martyrs' blood/life presented to God.

See also:
- Lev 4:18: "pour all the blood at the bottom of the altar of the burnt offering"
- Lev 4:25: "pour out his blood at the bottom of the altar of the burnt offering"
- Lev 4:30: "pour out all the blood thereof at the bottom of the altar"

---

### Exodus 29:12

> **29:12** And thou shalt take of the blood of the bullock, and put [it] upon the horns of the altar with thy finger, and pour all the blood beside the bottom of the altar.

**Notes:** Exodus 29:12 and Lev 4:7 together establish that the Levitical procedure was: (1) some blood on the horns; (2) the remainder poured at the base/bottom. The souls "under" the altar in Rev 6:9 correspond precisely to this Levitical blood-at-the-base imagery.

---

### Leviticus 17:11

> **17:11** For the life of the flesh [is] in the blood: and I have given it to you upon the altar to make an atonement for your souls: for it [is] the blood [that] maketh an atonement for the soul.

**Notes:** The nephesh (life/soul) of the flesh IS in the blood. This is the governing equation: soul = blood = life. When blood was poured at the altar's base, it was the soul/nephesh/life of the creature being offered. When Rev 6:9 describes "souls under the altar," it uses the Greek psychē (= nephesh) — and the Levitical background establishes that the "souls under the altar" are the lifeblood of the martyrs, poured out in sacrifice, now resting at the base of the heavenly altar.

---

### Leviticus 17:14

> **17:14** For [it is] the life of all flesh; the blood of it [is] for the life thereof: therefore I said unto the children of Israel, Ye shall eat the blood of no manner of flesh: for the life of all flesh [is] the blood thereof: whosoever eateth it shall be cut off.

**Notes:** Repeated identification: "the life of all flesh is the blood thereof." The nephesh = blood equation is not peripheral but central to Levitical theology. The Rev 6:9 "souls under the altar" = "blood of martyrs at the base of the altar" = the lifeblood of those sacrificed for their testimony.

---

### Hebrews 9:22

> **9:22** And almost all things are by the law purged with blood; and without shedding of blood is no remission.

**Notes:** The sacrificial system requires blood-shedding for atonement. The martyrs died as witnesses/sacrifices. Their blood/souls are at the altar of God, the place where blood atones. This connects the martyr's death to sacrificial offering.

## Section 7: State of the Dead / Rest Passages

### Ecclesiastes 9:5-6

> **9:5** For the living know that they shall die: but the dead know not any thing, neither have they any more a reward; for the memory of them is forgotten.
>
> **9:6** Also their love, and their hatred, and their envy, is now perished; neither have they any more a portion for ever in any [thing] that is done under the sun.

**Notes (E017 in ETC5):** The dead "know not anything." Their loves, hatreds, and envies "have perished." This is the state of the dead in the wisdom literature — no ongoing consciousness, no awareness of earthly events.

---

### Psalm 115:17

> **115:17** The dead praise not the LORD, neither any that go down into silence.

**Notes:** The dead do not praise the LORD — they are silent. This contrasts with the "crying" of the souls in Rev 6:10. Either: (a) the souls are an exception to this principle (requiring explanation), or (b) the "crying" of Rev 6:10 is figurative (consistent with Psa 115:17).

---

### Psalm 88:3-5, 11-12

> **88:3** For my soul is full of troubles: and my life draweth nigh unto the grave.
>
> **88:4** I am counted with them that go down into the pit: I am as a man [that hath] no strength:
>
> **88:5** Free among the dead, like the slain that lie in the grave, whom thou rememberest no more: and they are cut off from thy hand.
>
> **88:11** Shall thy lovingkindness be declared in the grave? [or] thy faithfulness in destruction?
>
> **88:12** Shall thy wonders be known in the dark? and thy righteousness in the land of forgetfulness?

**Notes:** The rhetorical questions of Ps 88:11-12 expect "no" as answers — lovingkindness is NOT declared in the grave; God's wonders are NOT known in "the land of forgetfulness." The dead are not conscious of God's activity. The grave is described as a place of silence and forgetting, not conscious waiting.

### Daniel 12:2

> **12:2** And many of them that sleep in the dust of the earth shall awake, some to everlasting life, and some to shame [and] everlasting contempt.

**Notes:** The dead "sleep in the dust" — dormancy, not consciousness. They "awake" at the resurrection — the awakening is the resurrection, not some intermediate state of conscious awareness.

---

### Daniel 12:13

> **12:13** But go thou thy way till the end [be]: for thou shalt rest, and stand in thy lot at the end of the days.

**Notes:** Daniel is told to "rest" (nuach — same semantic domain as anapauo). Then at "the end of the days" he will "stand in his lot." The sequence is: rest (death) → stand (resurrection/judgment). This is the rest-then-vindication pattern that exactly parallels Rev 6:11 ("rest yet for a little season, until... fulfilled") → Rev 20:4 (martyrs live at the first resurrection).

### 1 Thessalonians 4:13-17

> **4:13** But I would not have you to be ignorant, brethren, concerning them which are asleep, that ye sorrow not, even as others which have no hope.
>
> **4:14** For if we believe that Jesus died and rose again, even so them also which sleep in Jesus will God bring with him.
>
> **4:15** For this we say unto you by the word of the Lord, that we which are alive [and] remain unto the coming of the Lord shall not prevent them which are asleep.
>
> **4:16** For the Lord himself shall descend from heaven with a shout, with the voice of the archangel, and with the trump of God: and the dead in Christ shall rise first:
>
> **4:17** Then we which are alive [and] remain shall be caught up together with them in the clouds, to meet the Lord in the air: and so shall we ever be with the Lord.

**Notes:** Paul consistently uses "sleep" for the state of the dead (v.13,14,15). The moment of reunion with Christ is at "the coming of the Lord" (v.15), when "the dead in Christ shall rise first" (v.16). There is no description of an active intermediate state of consciousness; the dead sleep, and they rise at the resurrection.

### Revelation 20:4-6

> **20:4** And I saw thrones, and they sat upon them, and judgment was given unto them: and [I saw] the souls of them that were beheaded for the witness of Jesus, and for the word of God, and which had not worshipped the beast, neither his image, neither had received [his] mark upon their foreheads, or in their hands; and they lived and reigned with Christ a thousand years.
>
> **20:5** But the rest of the dead lived not again until the thousand years were finished. This [is] the first resurrection.
>
> **20:6** Blessed and holy [is] he that hath part in the first resurrection: on such the second death hath no power, but they shall be priests of God and of Christ, and shall reign with him a thousand years.

**Critical notes:**
- "The souls of them that were beheaded for the witness of Jesus, and for the word of God" (v.4) = identical vocabulary to Rev 6:9 ("souls of them that were slain... for the testimony which they held")
- In Rev 6:9: tas psychas tōn esphagmenōn dia ton logon tou theou kai dia tēn martyrian
- In Rev 20:4: tas psychas tōn pepelekismenōn dia tēn martyrian Iēsou kai dia ton logon tou theou
- Both passages use tas psychas ("the souls"), dia ton logon tou theou ("for the word of God"), dia tēn martyrian ("for the testimony")
- Rev 20:4 says: "they LIVED" (ezēsan — aorist indicative active, from zaō) — this "living" is explicitly called "the first resurrection" (v.5)
- Rev 20:5: "the rest of the dead LIVED NOT AGAIN until the thousand years were finished. This IS the first resurrection." The martyrs live (resurrect) at this point; "the rest of the dead" do not live until afterward.
- This passage locates the martyrs' "living" at the resurrection, not at death or in an intermediate state.

### Revelation 20:5 — Greek note

"οἱ λοιποὶ τῶν νεκρῶν οὐκ ἔζησαν ἄχρι τελεσθῇ τὰ χίλια ἔτη. αὕτη ἡ ἀνάστασις ἡ πρώτη."

Parsing:
- ἔζησαν (ezēsan): Aor Act Ind Pl 3rd — "they lived" (same form as in v.4)
- ἀνάστασις (anastasis) G386: "resurrection" — the standing up of the dead
- The sentence structure: the rest of the dead "did not live until..." — negating any life prior to the thousand years ending for them. The first resurrection applies specifically to the martyrs of v.4.

### Revelation 14:13

> **14:13** And I heard a voice from heaven saying unto me, Write, Blessed [are] the dead which die in the Lord from henceforth: Yea, saith the Spirit, that they may rest from their labours; and their works do follow them.

**Notes:**
- "The dead which die in the Lord" are "blessed" — but their blessedness is described as: "they may REST (anapauō) from their labours"
- anapauō (G373) is the same word used in Rev 6:11: "that they should REST yet for a little season"
- The Spirit affirms this rest as the condition of the blessed dead — not consciousness, not activity, not awareness, but rest
- "Their works do follow them" — their record accompanies them to judgment; they themselves rest

## Section 8: ECT-Supporting Martyrdom Passages (Rev 20:4; 2:10; 14:13)

### Revelation 2:10

> **2:10** Fear none of those things which thou shalt suffer: behold, the devil shall cast [some] of you into prison, that ye may be tried; and ye shall have tribulation ten days: be thou faithful unto death, and I will give thee a crown of life.

**Notes:** "Faithful unto death" — death is the point of maximum faithfulness. The crown of life is promised at the resurrection (cf. 1 Cor 9:25; 2 Tim 4:8; Jas 1:12; 1 Pet 5:4 — crown given by the Judge at the judgment). This does not require an intermediate conscious state; the crown is the resurrection reward.

### Additional martyrdom/blood passages

**Revelation 12:11**
> And they overcame him by the blood of the Lamb, and by the word of their testimony; and they loved not their lives unto the death.

"Loved not their lives (psychas) unto the death" — psychē = life, specifically physical life. They gave up biological life. This uses psychē in the same sense as nephesh = the living self that can be forfeited in death.

**Revelation 16:6**
> For they have shed the blood of saints and prophets, and thou hast given them blood to drink; for they are worthy.

Martyrs' blood = "the blood of saints and prophets." Blood stands for the martyrs themselves (metonymy). The judgment is blood-for-blood — those who shed blood receive blood to drink.

**Revelation 17:6**
> And I saw the woman drunken with the blood of the saints, and with the blood of the martyrs of Jesus: and when I saw her, I wondered with great admiration.

"Blood of the martyrs" — again, blood as metonymy for the martyrs themselves.

**Revelation 18:24**
> And in her was found the blood of prophets, and of saints, and of all that were slain upon the earth.

"Blood of all that were slain" — esphagmenōn (G4969, same word as Rev 6:9 "souls of them that were slain"). The blood/soul terminology consistently treats blood as representing the martyred persons.

**Revelation 19:1-2**
> (19:1) And after these things I heard a great voice of much people in heaven, saying, Alleluia; Salvation, and glory, and honour, and power, unto the Lord our God:
> (19:2) For true and righteous [are] his judgments: for he hath judged the great whore, which did corrupt the earth with her fornication, and hath avenged the blood of his servants at her hand.

The fulfillment: God has "avenged" (ekdikeō — same word as Rev 6:10) "the blood of his servants." This is the narrative completion of the Rev 6:10 cry. The arc: cry (6:10) → fulfillment (19:2). The vindication comes at the final judgment, not in an intermediate state.

## Additional Supporting Passages

### Romans 12:19
> Dearly beloved, avenge not yourselves, but [rather] give place unto wrath: for it is written, Vengeance [is] mine; I will repay, saith the Lord.

The same ekdikeo/ekdikesis framework — God will vindicate. The saints wait; God acts.

### Deuteronomy 32:43
> Rejoice, O ye nations, [with] his people: for he will avenge the blood of his servants, and will render vengeance to his adversaries, and will be merciful unto his land, [and] to his people.

The OT promise: God will avenge his servants' blood. This is the theological foundation for the Rev 6:10 cry.

### 2 Chronicles 24:22
> Thus Joash the king remembered not the kindness which Jehoiada his father had done to him, but slew his son. And when he died, he said, The LORD look upon [it], and require [it].

Zechariah (prophet, son of Jehoiada) as he dies says "The LORD look upon it, and require it" — a blood-cry made at the moment of death. Jesus references Zechariah in Matt 23:35.

### James 5:4
> Behold, the hire of the labourers who have reaped down your fields, which is of you kept back by fraud, crieth: and the cries of them which have reaped are entered into the ears of the Lord of sabaoth.

The withheld wages "crieth" (krazō — same root as Rev 6:10 ekraxan). Inanimate money/wages cries out for justice. This is another instance of the crying-for-justice personification that does not require the speaker to be conscious.

### Luke 19:40
> And he answered and said unto them, I tell you that, if these should hold their peace, the stones would immediately cry out.

Stones would "cry out" — clearly inanimate objects do not literally cry, but Jesus uses the figurative convention of "crying" as a demand for acknowledgment of truth. This confirms the figurative range of "crying" in NT usage.

### Romans 8:22
> For we know that the whole creation groaneth and travaileth in pain together until now.

Creation "groans" (stenazō) — personification of the created order longing for redemption. This is the same literary mode as Rev 6:10.

### Psalm 79:10
> Wherefore should the heathen say, Where [is] their God? let him be known among the heathen in our sight [by] the revenging of the blood of thy servants [which is] shed.

Blood-avenging as divine vindication of the slain — same structure as Rev 6:10.

### Hebrews 11:13, 39-40
> (11:13) These all died in faith, not having received the promises, but having seen them afar off, and were persuaded of [them], and embraced [them], and confessed that they were strangers and pilgrims on the earth.
> (11:39) And these all, having obtained a good report through faith, received not the promise:
> (11:40) God having provided some better thing for us, that they without us should not be made perfect.

The patriarchs "died in faith, not having received the promises" — they await the promises at the resurrection. They are "not yet made perfect" without us. This frames the faithful dead as waiting, not as already experiencing the fullness of the promise.

### Revelation 8:9 — psychē in biological context
> And the third part of the creatures which were in the sea, and had life, died; and the third part of the ships were destroyed.

"Had life" — psychē (G5590) here = biological life of sea creatures. This establishes that psychē in Revelation is used for biological life, not disembodied spirits.

### Revelation 16:3 — psychē in biological context
> And the second angel poured out his vial upon the sea; and it became as the blood of a dead [man]: and every living soul died in the sea.

"Every living soul" — psychē as biological life of sea creatures. Again: psychē = living creature, biological life, not a disembodied spirit.

### Revelation 18:13 — psychē as persons/slaves
> And cinnamon, and odours, and ointments, and frankincense, and wine, and oil, and fine flour, and wheat, and beasts, and sheep, and horses, and chariots, and slaves, and souls of men.

"Souls of men" — psychai anthrōpōn = persons, human beings as commercial commodities (slavery). Psychē here is a person, not a disembodied spirit.

### Revelation 18:14 — psychē as desire/appetite
> And the fruits that thy soul lusted after are departed from thee, and all things which were dainty and goodly are departed from thee, and thou shalt find them no more at all.

"Thy soul lusted after" — psychē = appetite, desire. This further widens the semantic range of psychē in Revelation beyond "disembodied spirit."
