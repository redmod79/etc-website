# Verse Analysis
## Study: etc5-02-who-has-immortality
## Date: 2026-02-19

---

## Question

Who possesses immortality according to Scripture? Is the human soul inherently immortal or is immortality a divine gift? What words does the Bible use for immortality/incorruption? When and how does a human receive immortality? What is the relationship between immortality and the resurrection?

## 1. The Divine Exclusive Claim

### 1 Timothy 6:16

**Context:** Paul's charge to Timothy in 6:11-19. Verse 15 calls God "the blessed and only [monos] Potentate, the King of kings, and Lord of lords." Verse 16 follows: "Who only [monos] hath immortality [athanasian], dwelling in the light which no man can approach unto."

**What the text says:**
- God "only" (monos) has (echo, present active participle — continuous, permanent possession) immortality (athanasia, G110 = deathlessness)
- The same word monos appears twice in vv.15-16: God is the "only Potentate" and the one who "only hath immortality"
- Timothy is instructed in v.12 to "lay hold on eternal life" — a command to grasp what he does not yet possess
- God dwells in unapproachable light; no man has seen or can see him

**What the text does NOT say:**
- It does not say God has immortality in a comparative or pre-eminent sense while others have a lesser immortality
- It does not qualify monos as "uniquely pre-eminent" rather than "exclusively"
- It does not say this is a statement about God's immortality relative to fallen humans only — the statement is unqualified

**Key observations:**
- G110 (athanasia) occurs only 3 times in the NT. Two of those occurrences are in 1 Cor 15:53-54, where mortals must "put on" immortality at the resurrection. The third is here, where God alone "has" it as a present possession. The distribution of the word itself tells the story: God possesses it; mortals must receive it at a future event.
- The present participle "having" (echon) indicates a continuous, inherent state. God does not receive or put on immortality — he simply, permanently "has" it.
- The grammar of monos (alone/only) functions as a substantival adjective — it is not a comparative intensifier but an exclusive marker.

**Cross-reference to etc5-01:** This is E29 in the prior study (Master E029). N5 (God currently possesses immortality exclusively; no creature currently possesses it as an inherent attribute) is a necessary implication established in that study. This study will confirm and deepen that finding.

---

### 1 Timothy 1:17

**Context:** A spontaneous doxology by Paul at the end of his reflection on God's mercy to him (1:12-17). Paul has just described how Christ revealed grace to "them which should hereafter believe on him to life everlasting" (v.16).

**What the text says:**
- God is "King eternal [aionion], immortal [aphtharto, G862], invisible, the only wise God"
- This verse applies G862 (aphthartos = incorruptible, undecaying) directly to God as a divine attribute
- The context connects God's eternal kingship with his incorruptibility

**What the text does NOT say:**
- This verse does not use G110 (athanasia); it uses G862 (aphthartos), the adjectival form of incorruption
- It does not say humans share this attribute

**Key observations:**
- 1 Tim 1:17 and 1 Tim 6:16 are in the same letter by the same author and together establish two complementary facts: God is "immortal" (aphthartos, 1:17) and God "alone has immortality" (monos echon athanasian, 6:16). Within the single letter, Paul both applies the divine attribute and declares its exclusivity.
- The word aphthartos (G862) is also used in Rom 1:23 ("the glory of the uncorruptible God") — same attribute, different context. Humans are there described as phthartos (corruptible, G5349) — the explicit antonym.

---

### John 5:26

**Context:** Jesus's discourse on his authority in John 5:19-29, specifically after healing the man at the Pool of Bethesda. Jesus addresses the relationship between Father and Son.

**What the text says:**
- "For as the Father hath life in himself [zoe en heauto]; so hath he given to the Son to have life in himself [zoe en heauto]"
- "Life in himself" is a property originating with the Father
- The Father "gave" this property to the Son — the Son's possession of it is derived, not independently originating, but equally real

**What the text does NOT say:**
- It does not say humans have "life in themselves"
- It does not say this life-in-oneself is something believers receive now or at death
- The text specifically locates this attribute in the divine persons, not in human beings

**Key observations:**
- "Life in himself" (zoe en heauto) is the Greek NT's closest equivalent to what theologians call aseity applied to life — self-existence of life. This stands in direct contrast to the human condition, which requires a source external to itself for life (Gen 2:7: the breath of God; John 3:16: eternal life through the Son).
- The sequence in 5:28-29 (all who are in graves shall come forth at the voice of the Son) shows that the Son's possession of "life in himself" enables him to raise the dead — it is the ground of resurrection, not something humans share in now.
- This passage provides the theological background for why 1 Tim 6:16 can say God alone has immortality: "life in himself" is a divine attribute that God shares within the Trinity but not with creation.

## 2. The Current Human Condition: Mortal and Corruptible

### Romans 6:12 and 8:11

**What the text says:**
- Rom 6:12: "Let not sin therefore reign in your mortal [thneto, G2349] body" — the present body is described as mortal
- Rom 8:11: "He that raised up Christ from the dead shall also quicken your mortal [thneta, G2349] bodies by his Spirit that dwelleth in you" — the quickening of mortal bodies is a future promise, not a present state

**What the text does NOT say:**
- Neither verse says the soul within the mortal body is immortal
- Rom 8:11 does not say the Spirit is currently immortalizing the body — the "shall also quicken" is a future promise

**Key observations:**
- G2349 (thnetos, mortal) is applied to the present human body in both verses. Rom 8:11 shows that mortal bodies become quickened — but this quickening is future ("shall quicken"), tied to the resurrection agency of the same Spirit who raised Christ.
- In Rom 8:21-23, Paul says the whole creation groans under "the bondage of corruption" and believers themselves groan waiting for "the redemption of our body" (v.23). The body-redemption is not yet accomplished; it is awaited. This is the present human condition: under mortality and corruption, awaiting transformation.

---

### 2 Corinthians 4:11 and 5:1-4

**What the text says:**
- 2 Cor 4:11: "The life also of Jesus might be made manifest in our mortal [thnetei, G2349] flesh" — even while experiencing Christ's life, the flesh is described as "mortal"
- 2 Cor 5:4: "that mortality [to thneton, G2349 as noun] might be swallowed up of life" — mortality is the present condition; being "swallowed up of life" is the desired future outcome
- 2 Cor 5:1-4: Paul describes groaning "in this tabernacle" (the present mortal body), desiring not to be "unclothed" (dead) but "clothed upon" (transformed at the resurrection)

**What the text does NOT say:**
- 2 Cor 5:4 does not say the soul will escape the mortal body and thereby be immortal
- The passage does not present death as the solution to mortality — Paul explicitly says he does NOT want to be "unclothed" (naked = bodiless), but wants to be "clothed upon" (resurrection transformation)

**Key observations:**
- The clothing metaphor in 2 Cor 5:1-4 parallels 1 Cor 15:53-54 ("put on immortality"). Both use the language of being clothed with a new state rather than being released from a body. The goal is not soul-escape but transformation.
- "Mortality swallowed up of life" (2 Cor 5:4) is a direct lexical parallel to "Death is swallowed up in victory" (1 Cor 15:54, quoting Isa 25:8). Paul is using the same eschatological language in both letters.

---

### Romans 1:23

**What the text says:**
- "They changed the glory of the uncorruptible [aphthartou, G862] God into an image made like to corruptible [phtharto, G5349] man, and to birds, and fourfooted beasts, and creeping things"

**What the text does NOT say:**
- It does not say humans are partially incorruptible or have an incorruptible soul within a corruptible body
- It does not distinguish the human body from the human soul in this verse

**Key observations:**
- Paul categorizes humans together with animals as phthartos (corruptible). The explicit contrast is between the aphthartos (incorruptible) God and phthartos (corruptible) man. This verse presents the incorruptibility-vs-corruptibility contrast as a Creator-creature distinction, not a body-soul distinction within the creature.
- This is significant for the ECT claim that the soul is incorruptible: Paul does not make that distinction here. He classifies humans as corruptible, full stop, as a category.

---

### Hebrews 9:27

**What the text says:**
- "It is appointed unto men once to die, but after this the judgment"

**What the text does NOT say:**
- It does not say death is followed by an immediate resurrection or immediate final state
- It does not say the soul lives on between death and judgment
- It does not say death is followed by any intermediate conscious experience

**Key observations:**
- "Appointed unto men once to die" — death is the universal and definitive human experience; it is divinely appointed (apokeimai = reserved/destined). This is not the exception but the rule for all humans.
- "After this the judgment" — the sequence is death → judgment. The text says what comes after death (judgment), without specifying any intermediate state. It is not compatible with either "immediate conscious existence" or "unconscious sleep" as a specific teaching — it simply does not address the interval.

## 3. The Tree of Life: Eden and Eschatology

### Genesis 2:9

**What the text says:**
- The LORD God caused to grow "the tree of life also in the midst of the garden"
- The tree of life (ets hachayim) was planted in Eden — an external object in the garden

**What the text does NOT say:**
- It does not say man had inherent immortality independent of the tree
- It does not say man was forbidden from the tree before the fall

**Key observations:**
- The tree of life was accessible to man before the fall (no prohibition was placed on it — only the tree of knowledge was prohibited, Gen 2:17). Man's pre-fall condition appears to have included potential access to endless life via the tree — not as an inherent property but as a conditional access point.
- This suggests man was not inherently immortal even before the fall; immortality was conditioned on continued access to the tree.

---

### Genesis 3:22-24

**What the text says:**
- "And now, lest he put forth his hand, and take also of the tree of life, and eat, and live for ever [wachay le-olam]"
- God expelled man and placed cherubim and a flaming sword to keep "the way of the tree of life" — to prevent access

**What the text does NOT say:**
- God does not say man already has immortality; the concern is that man might ACQUIRE it
- The text does not say man had immortality before the fall that was now lost — it says man might gain ("take also") immortality that he does not yet have

**Key observations:**
- The phrasing "lest he... eat, and live for ever" establishes that immortality was NOT man's present state after the fall. God's concern is future acquisition, not present possession. The action required for immortality is eating the tree — an external act, not an internal faculty.
- Hebrew: "wachay le-olam" (וָחַי לְעֹלָם) = "and live for eternity." This is the exact Hebrew equivalent of the NT's "life eternal" / "everlasting life." The tree of life was the OT mechanism; faith in Christ is the NT mechanism. Both are external sources.
- The cherubim and flaming sword are a physical barrier preventing access to the tree. God does not destroy the tree — he blocks access to it. This foreshadows the eschatological restoration: the tree reappears in Rev 22.
- **Cross-reference to etc5-01:** E3 in the prior study (Master E003) = "God bars access to the tree of life to prevent man from eating 'and live for ever.'" This passage was already classified as a Conditionalist E-tier item. This study confirms it.

---

### Revelation 2:7

**What the text says:**
- "To him that overcometh will I give to eat of the tree of life, which is in the midst of the paradise of God"

**What the text does NOT say:**
- It does not say overcomers currently possess immortality
- It does not say this is a metaphor for a spiritual state they already experience

**Key observations:**
- The promise is future and conditional: "to him that overcometh" (future-oriented, victory-requiring) "will I give" (future gift). The tree of life is a future eschatological reward.
- "The paradise of God" echoes Eden (Gen 2-3) and the new creation (Rev 22). The bookend structure is: tree of life in Eden (accessible) → tree blocked after fall → tree promised to overcomers → tree restored in new Jerusalem (Rev 22:2).

---

### Revelation 22:2, 14

**What the text says:**
- Rev 22:2: "the tree of life, which bare twelve manner of fruits, and yielded her fruit every month: and the leaves of the tree were for the healing of the nations"
- Rev 22:14: "Blessed are they that do his commandments, that they may have right to the tree of life, and may enter in through the gates into the city"

**What the text does NOT say:**
- The tree of life in the new Jerusalem is not currently accessible — it is a future promise
- The "right to the tree" is described as a blessing, not a present entitlement

**Key observations:**
- The tree of life at the conclusion of Revelation is the eschatological answer to the tree barred in Gen 3:24. The narrative arc runs: immortality-conditional-on-tree (Gen 2) → tree blocked (Gen 3) → tree promised to overcomers (Rev 2:7) → tree restored in the new creation (Rev 22).
- Perpetual fruitfulness and healing of nations suggest this is not figurative but literally describes the eternal state. The fact that "there shall be no more curse" (Rev 22:3) indicates the Gen 3 curse is finally reversed — including the mortality it introduced.

## 4. When and How Immortality Is Received: The Resurrection

### 1 Corinthians 15:42-54

**Context:** This is the climactic section of Paul's resurrection argument, addressing the question "with what body do they come?" (v.35). Paul uses the seed-and-body analogy (vv.36-41) and then applies it to the resurrection.

**What the text says:**
- v.42: "It is sown in corruption [phthora]; it is raised in incorruption [aphtharsia, G861]"
- v.43: "Sown in dishonour... raised in glory; sown in weakness... raised in power"
- v.44: "Sown a natural [psychikos] body; raised a spiritual [pneumatikos] body"
- v.50: "Flesh and blood cannot inherit the kingdom of God; neither doth corruption [phthora] inherit incorruption [aphtharsia]"
- v.51-52: The dead shall be "raised incorruptible [aphthartoi, G862]" and the living "shall be changed" — "in a moment, in the twinkling of an eye, at the last trump"
- v.53: "For this corruptible [phtharton, G5349] must put on incorruption [aphtharsian, G861], and this mortal [thneton, G2349] must put on immortality [athanasian, G110]"
- v.54: "So when this corruptible shall have put on incorruption, and this mortal shall have put on immortality, then shall be brought to pass the saying that is written, Death is swallowed up in victory"

**What the text does NOT say:**
- Paul does not say the soul has already put on immortality at death or at regeneration
- Paul does not say "this corruptible body must be shed so the immortal soul can be free"
- The text does not present death as the acquisition of immortality — it presents the resurrection as the acquisition of immortality
- Paul does not distinguish between the soul and body here — "this mortal" refers to the whole person in its present mortal condition

**Key observations:**
- The four paired contrasts in vv.42-44 (corruption/incorruption, dishonour/glory, weakness/power, natural/spiritual) all describe what is SOWN vs. what is RAISED. The subject of these contrasts is the same entity in two states — before and after resurrection. This is transformation, not liberation of soul from body.
- The "must" (dei, G1163) in v.53 indicates divine necessity. The mortal MUST put on immortality — it has not yet done so. The "must" points to an unfulfilled obligation that will be accomplished at the resurrection event.
- The timing is explicit: v.52 says "at the last trump." The putting on of immortality happens at a specific eschatological moment, not at death.
- The two parallel pairs in v.53:
  - phthartos (corruptible, G5349) → aphtharsia (incorruption, G861) = body transformation
  - thnetos (mortal, G2349) → athanasia (immortality, G110) = life transformation
  Both transformations happen simultaneously at the resurrection.
- Paul quotes Isa 25:8 in v.54 ("Death is swallowed up in victory") and Hos 13:14 in v.55. These OT texts were promises about God's future victory over death. Paul declares them fulfilled at the resurrection. The resurrection is the event of immortality-reception; the OT pointed forward to it.
- **Cross-reference to etc5-01:** E31-E32 (Master E031-E032), E54 (Master E054), N6 (Master N006), N10 (Master N010) are all directly established by this passage. This study provides deeper word-study grounding for those already-established items.

### Luke 20:35-36

**What the text says:**
- "They which shall be accounted worthy to obtain that world, and the resurrection from the dead, neither marry, nor are given in marriage"
- "Neither can they die any more: for they are equal unto the angels; and are the children of God, being the children of the resurrection"

**What the text does NOT say:**
- It does not say humans currently cannot die
- It does not say the present human soul is deathless
- It does not say all who die go to a state where they "cannot die any more"

**Key observations:**
- The condition "cannot die any more" is explicitly tied to "that world" and "the resurrection from the dead." The deathless state is post-resurrection, not present.
- "Being the children of the resurrection" — the deathless status is derived from and dependent on the resurrection event, not from a pre-existing immortal soul.
- The angel-comparison is significant: angels do not reproduce because they are not subject to generational replacement (death requiring new births). Post-resurrection humans will share this characteristic — but only post-resurrection.

---

### Philippians 3:20-21

**What the text says:**
- "Who shall change [metaschematizo, future] our vile body [soma tes tapeinoseos = body of our lowliness/humiliation], that it may be fashioned like unto his glorious body [soma tes doxes]"

**What the text does NOT say:**
- The text does not say the soul is currently fashioned like Christ's glorified state
- The transformation is future ("shall change") and involves the body

**Key observations:**
- "Vile body" (soma tes tapeinoseos) = the present human body in its state of humiliation/lowliness — the same mortal, corruptible body described throughout Paul's letters. This body is to be transformed, not discarded.
- The transformation is into the likeness of Christ's resurrection body — a body that is already glorified, immortal, incorruptible. The future believers' state mirrors Christ's present state.

---

### 1 Thessalonians 4:13-18

**What the text says:**
- "Them which are asleep" (v.13) — deceased believers are described as sleeping
- "Them also which sleep in Jesus will God bring with him" (v.14)
- "The dead in Christ shall rise first" (v.16) — resurrection precedes the gathering of living believers
- "Then we which are alive and remain shall be caught up together with them" (v.17)

**What the text does NOT say:**
- It does not say the dead in Christ are currently in a state of conscious existence with the Lord
- It does not say the resurrection is irrelevant because the dead are already with Christ
- The comfort offered is the resurrection, not the intermediate state: v.18 "Wherefore comfort one another with these words" — referring to the resurrection sequence just described

**Key observations:**
- The sequence: (1) Lord descends; (2) dead in Christ rise first; (3) living believers caught up together with them. The resurrection of the dead is the critical step — the dead are currently in the sleep of death, not in ongoing conscious fellowship with Christ.
- Paul offers the resurrection as comfort for bereaved believers. If the dead were already consciously present with Christ in a better state, the comfort offered would logically be "they are already with Christ now, and resurrection will complete that." Instead, Paul's comfort is entirely focused on the resurrection event as the reunification point.
- "Sleep in Jesus" (v.14) uses the sleep metaphor that is established across both testaments (etc5-01, N9 / Master N009).

## 5. Christ as the Agent of Immortality

### 2 Timothy 1:9-10

**What the text says:**
- God's purpose and grace was "given us in Christ Jesus before the world began"
- Jesus Christ "hath abolished [katargeo, aorist participle = completed/accomplished action] death"
- Jesus Christ "hath brought life and immortality [aphtharsian, G861] to light through the gospel"

**What the text does NOT say:**
- It does not say immortality was always a present human possession that Christ merely revealed
- It does not say the gospel reveals that humans are immortal

**Key observations:**
- "Abolished death" — katargeo means to render inoperative, nullify, destroy the effectiveness of. Death as a power has been defeated in Christ's resurrection. But the full implementation of this abolition awaits the resurrection of believers (1 Cor 15:26 — "the last enemy that shall be destroyed is death").
- "Brought to light" (photizo, aorist participle) — the life and immortality were part of the divine plan "before the world began" (v.9) but were hidden. Christ's appearing made them accessible and visible. This is not "revealing that humans always had immortality" but "bringing to availability the immortality that was God's purpose from eternity."
- G861 (aphtharsia) is the word for immortality here, as in Rom 2:7. In both cases it is something humans seek (Rom 2:7) or something Christ brings to light (2 Tim 1:10) — not something they already possess.

---

### John 3:15-16, 36

**What the text says:**
- "Whosoever believeth in him should not perish, but have eternal life" (v.15)
- "Whosoever believeth in him should not perish, but have everlasting life" (v.16)
- "He that believeth not the Son shall not see life; but the wrath of God abideth on him" (v.36)

**What the text does NOT say:**
- It does not say all humans have eternal life regardless of belief
- It does not say "eternal life" is merely the continuation of an immortal soul everyone already possesses

**Key observations:**
- The contrast "not perish / have everlasting life" is foundational. The alternative to eternal life is perishing (apollumi, G622 = to destroy, perish, be ruined). The contrast structure (not-perish vs. have-life) implies that the default state — without belief in the Son — is perishing, not eternal existence in any form.
- "Shall not see life" (v.36) — the unbeliever does not see life; the wrath of God "abideth" (present tense, ongoing) on him. Life is something to be seen, received — not a universal endowment.
- These verses establish the conditional structure of eternal life: it is contingent on relationship with the Son (explicitly stated in 1 John 5:11-12).

---

### John 10:28

**What the text says:**
- "I give unto them eternal life; and they shall never perish, neither shall any man pluck them out of my hand"

**What the text does NOT say:**
- It does not say eternal life is an inherent human property that Christ merely protects
- It does not say all humans receive this gift

**Key observations:**
- "I give" — eternal life is Christ's to give; it is a gift from him, not an inherent possession. The sheep receive it from him.
- "Never perish" — the "never perish" is a consequence of receiving eternal life from Christ. It is not a statement about human nature independent of Christ.

---

### John 11:25-26

**What the text says:**
- "I am the resurrection, and the life: he that believeth in me, though he were dead, yet shall he live"
- "Whosoever liveth and believeth in me shall never die"

**What the text does NOT say:**
- It does not say all humans shall never die regardless of faith
- The "shall never die" is conditional: "whosoever liveth and believeth in me"

**Key observations:**
- The resurrection is located IN CHRIST — "I am the resurrection." Not: "there is a faculty in humans called resurrection." The resurrection is a divine capacity that Christ embodies.
- Martha's response (v.24: "I know that he shall rise again in the resurrection at the last day") shows she understood the correct framework: resurrection at the last day. Jesus affirms her understanding by placing the resurrection in himself.
- "Though he were dead, yet shall he live" — even physical death is overridden by Christ's resurrection power. This does not say the dead continue to live between death and resurrection; it says Christ will raise them.

---

### 1 John 5:11-12

**What the text says:**
- "God hath given to us eternal life, and this life is in his Son"
- "He that hath the Son hath life; and he that hath not the Son of God hath not life"

**What the text does NOT say:**
- The verse does not say "eternal life is in human nature"; it says it is in the Son
- The negative — "hath not the Son hath not life" — is absolute and unqualified

**Key observations:**
- The exclusivity is precise: life is in the Son. Having the Son = having life. Not having the Son = not having life. This is the most concentrated statement of the conditional and Christocentric nature of eternal life in the NT.
- "Hath not life" (ouk echei ten zoen) — the unbeliever currently does not possess life (eternal life). The present tense "hath not" describes the present state, not just the future outcome.
- This passage provides the theological foundation for understanding why 1 Tim 6:16 says God "alone has immortality" — within the created order, life (eternal life) is exclusively in the Son, given by the Father; it does not belong to creatures independently.

## 6. Immortality as Gift/Inheritance

### Romans 6:23

**What the text says:**
- "The wages of sin is death; but the gift of God is eternal life through Jesus Christ our Lord"

**What the text does NOT say:**
- It does not say the wages of sin is eternal conscious torment — the stated wage is death
- It does not say humans possess eternal life apart from Christ

**Key observations:**
- The structure is explicitly contrastive: the result of sin is death (thanatos, G2288); the gift of God is eternal life (zoe aionios). These are the two outcomes: death or eternal life.
- "Wages" (opsonia, earned income) vs. "gift" (charisma, grace-gift) — death is what sin earns; eternal life cannot be earned, it is given. This contrast reinforces both that death is the default human fate (the earned result of sin) and that life is a gift from outside the human person.
- **Cross-reference to etc5-01:** This passage contributes to the Conditionalist picture established in etc5-01 (death as the earned outcome; life as the given outcome).

---

### Romans 2:7

**What the text says:**
- "To them who by patient continuance in well doing seek for glory and honour and immortality [aphtharsian, G861], eternal life"

**What the text does NOT say:**
- It does not say immortality is currently possessed
- It does not say seeking immortality is seeking something already within human nature

**Key observations:**
- "Seek" (zetousin, Present Active Participle) = ongoing, active seeking. One does not seek what one already possesses.
- Immortality here is listed alongside "eternal life" as a sought-after goal — suggesting these are synonymous or closely related concepts. Both are future, both are sought, both are not yet possessed.
- **Cross-reference to etc5-01:** This is E30 (Master E030), N12 (Master N012) in the prior study.

---

### 1 Peter 1:3-5

**What the text says:**
- Believers are "begotten again unto a lively hope by the resurrection of Jesus Christ"
- The inheritance is "incorruptible [aphtharton, G862], and undefiled, and that fadeth not away, reserved in heaven for you"
- The inheritance is "ready to be revealed in the last time" — it is currently reserved, not yet received

**What the text does NOT say:**
- The inheritance is not currently in the believer's possession
- The "incorruptible" nature of the inheritance is not the same as the current believer being incorruptible

**Key observations:**
- Three descriptors of the inheritance: aphthartos (incorruptible), amianton (undefiled), amarantos (unfading/unchanging). These stand in contrast to everything in the present corruptible order.
- "Kept by the power of God through faith unto salvation ready to be revealed in the last time" — the inheritance is being kept in heaven; the believer is being kept on earth by God's power. Both are in a "not yet" state with respect to the inheritance. The incorruptible inheritance is reserved for a future reception.
- This inheritance is grounded in the resurrection of Jesus Christ (v.3) — not in human nature. It is a new-birth inheritance, not a birthright of human nature.

---

### Romans 5:12-21

**What the text says:**
- "By one man sin entered into the world, and death by sin; and so death passed upon all men, for that all have sinned" (v.12)
- "Death reigned from Adam to Moses" (v.14)
- "As in Adam all die, even so in Christ shall all be made alive" (1 Cor 15:22, thematically parallel)
- "Sin hath reigned unto death, even so might grace reign through righteousness unto eternal life by Jesus Christ our Lord" (v.21)

**What the text does NOT say:**
- It does not say some spiritual part of humans escaped Adam's mortality
- It does not say the soul was exempt from the death that came through Adam

**Key observations:**
- "Death passed upon all men" — universal scope. Death entered through sin; it spread to all humans because all sinned. Paul does not exempt any part of human constitution from this passage of death.
- "Death reigned" (past tense) — death operated as a ruling power over all humanity. This describes not just physical death but the dominion of death as a condition.
- The remedy ("in Christ shall all be made alive") is the resurrection — a future event — not a present soul-liberation.
- The Adam-Christ typology establishes the framework: death through Adam (universal human condition) → life through Christ (received through union with him). The life is as dependent on Christ as the death is on Adam.

---

## 7. The Soul's Mortality

### Ezekiel 18:4, 20, 27

**What the text says:**
- v.4: "The soul [nephesh] that sinneth, it shall die [tamut, H4191]"
- v.20: "The soul that sinneth, it shall die" (repeated for emphasis)
- v.27: "When the wicked man turneth away from his wickedness... he shall save his soul alive" — the soul's life can be saved from dying

**What the text does NOT say:**
- The text does not say "only the body dies while the soul survives"
- The grammatical subject of tamut (shall die) is nephesh (soul), not the body

**Key observations:**
- The nephesh is the explicit grammatical subject of the death predicate. In Hebrew grammar, the soul is what dies in Ezekiel 18:4. This is a direct, unambiguous statement.
- The repetition in vv.4 and 20 (same statement twice in the same chapter) is a Hebrew emphasis device. The prophet is making a foundational principle, not a peripheral observation.
- "Save his soul alive" (v.27) uses the same logical structure: the soul would die if not saved. The saving of the soul is from death, not from bodily suffering.
- **Cross-reference to etc5-01:** This is E24 (Master E024), N7 (Master N007).

---

### Matthew 10:28

**What the text says:**
- "Fear not them which kill the body, but are not able to kill the soul: but rather fear him which is able to destroy both soul [psyche] and body [soma] in hell [gehenna]"

**What the text does NOT say:**
- It does not say the soul is immortal; it says men cannot kill it — God can
- It does not say the soul will survive gehenna — it says God can destroy both in gehenna

**Key observations:**
- The verse establishes three facts: (1) Human beings can kill the body. (2) Human beings cannot kill the soul. (3) God can destroy both soul and body in gehenna.
- The conditionalist reading: the soul is not beyond destruction — only beyond human destruction. God's power to destroy both soul and body is the reason to fear God.
- The ECT reading: the soul's exemption from human killing indicates its inherently indestructible nature; "destroy" in gehenna means render wretched/torment rather than annihilate.
- The text itself says God is "able to destroy" (apolesai, aorist infinitive of apollumi = to destroy, kill, render useless, bring to ruin) "both soul and body." The word apollumi is used elsewhere for perishing, loss, and destruction — including John 3:16 ("should not perish"). The text provides no qualifier limiting "destroy" to something other than destruction.
- **Cross-reference to etc5-01:** This is E26-E27 (Master E026-E027), N8 (Master N008).

---

### Revelation 16:3

**What the text says:**
- "The second angel poured out his vial upon the sea; and it became as the blood of a dead man: and every living soul [pasa psyche zoes] died in the sea"

**What the text does NOT say:**
- It does not say souls cannot die
- In apocalyptic context this likely refers to aquatic creatures (sea creatures)

**Key observations:**
- pasa psyche zoes = "every living soul-of-life" — the LXX construction identical to nephesh chayyah for aquatic creatures (Gen 1:20). This connects to the creation language for sea creatures.
- The aorist indicative "died" (apethanen) applies to psyche (soul) as the grammatical subject. Whether referring to sea creatures or humans, the passage confirms that psyche/soul can be the subject of dying — the soul can die.
- **Cross-reference to etc5-01:** This is E28 (Master E028).

## 8. OT Anticipation of Resurrection and Immortality

### Daniel 12:2-3, 13

**What the text says:**
- v.2: "Many of them that sleep [yishene] in the dust of the earth shall awake, some to everlasting life [chayyei olam], and some to shame and everlasting contempt [direaon olam]"
- v.3: "They that be wise shall shine as the brightness of the firmament; and they that turn many to righteousness as the stars for ever and ever"
- v.13: Daniel told: "thou shalt rest [nuach = lie still / sleep], and stand in thy lot at the end of the days"

**What the text does NOT say:**
- It does not say the dead are currently conscious in any realm
- It does not say some awake immediately to life and some to contempt — the awakening is a single future event
- Daniel is told to "rest" until the end — not to enter an immediate state of conscious bliss

**Key observations:**
- "Those sleeping in the dust of the earth" — the state of the dead is described as sleep in the dust, echoing Gen 3:19 (dust to dust), Gen 2:7 (formed from dust), and the sleep-metaphor for death.
- Two destinies from one resurrection event: chayyei olam (everlasting life) and direaon olam (everlasting contempt). The olam (eternity/forever, H5769) applies to BOTH outcomes.
- The Hebrew chayyei olam (life of eternity) becomes zoe aionios (eternal life) in the LXX — the exact phrase throughout John's Gospel. This is the OT seed of the NT concept.
- Daniel 12:13 is particularly significant: Daniel personally is told to "rest" (lie down/sleep) until "the end of the days." This is not "your soul will go to heaven when you die" — it is "you will lie in rest until the final resurrection." The patriarch himself is given the sleep-until-resurrection framework.
- **Cross-reference to etc5-01:** E23 (Master E023) — the prior study already classified Dan 12:2 as Neutral.

---

### Isaiah 25:7-8

**What the text says:**
- "He will destroy in this mountain the face of the covering cast over all people"
- "He will swallow up death in victory [lanetsach]; and the Lord GOD will wipe away tears from off all faces"

**What the text does NOT say:**
- It does not say death is currently swallowed up
- The "swallowing up" of death is a future eschatological event on "this mountain"

**Key observations:**
- The "vail spread over all nations" (v.7) and the "swallowing up of death" (v.8) are eschatological events — things God will do in a future day of salvation.
- Paul explicitly quotes v.8 in 1 Cor 15:54 ("then shall be brought to pass the saying that is written, Death is swallowed up in victory"). The OT promise is fulfilled AT the resurrection.
- "He will swallow up death in victory" — death is treated as an active enemy that God will defeat. The present reality is that death exists and reigns; the future promise is its destruction.

---

### Isaiah 26:14, 19

**What the text says:**
- v.14: "[The wicked dead] are dead, they shall not live; they are deceased, they shall not rise: therefore hast thou visited and destroyed them"
- v.19: "Thy dead men shall live, together with my dead body shall they arise. Awake and sing, ye that dwell in dust: for thy dew is as the dew of herbs, and the earth shall cast out the dead"

**What the text does NOT say:**
- v.14 does not say the wicked dead are in conscious torment — it says they "shall not live" and "shall not rise"
- v.19 does not say the righteous dead are currently alive in heaven — it says "shall live" and "shall arise" (future tense)

**Key observations:**
- The contrast between vv.14 and 19 is stark: the wicked dead "shall not live / shall not rise" vs. God's dead "shall live / shall arise." This is a statement about two destinies from the same resurrection premise.
- "Dwell in dust" (v.19) echoes Dan 12:2 — the state of the dead is characterized by dwelling in the dust, awaiting the call to arise.
- Isaiah 26:19 anticipates a physical resurrection — not a soul-ascent. The earth will "cast out" (naphal) the dead.

---

### Hosea 13:14

**What the text says:**
- "I will ransom them from the power of the grave [sheol]; I will redeem them from death: O death, I will be thy plagues; O grave, I will be thy destruction"

**What the text does NOT say:**
- It does not say the dead are not in the grave / sheol — it says they ARE in the grave (power of the grave) and God will ransom them FROM it
- It does not present sheol as a conscious, pleasant state

**Key observations:**
- The dead are in the "power of the grave" (sheol) — sheol has authority over them until God acts to ransom them. The dead are not presented as free from sheol but as under its power.
- God will be sheol's "destruction" — God intends to eliminate the power of death/sheol, not merely allow souls to escape it.
- Paul applies this text in 1 Cor 15:55 ("O death, where is thy sting? O grave, where is thy victory?") to the resurrection triumph. The OT promise becomes the resurrection triumph cry.

---

### Psalms 49:7-9, 14-15

**What the text says:**
- "None of them can by any means redeem his brother, nor give to God a ransom for him" (v.7)
- "That he should still live for ever [chay le-olam], and not see corruption" (v.9)
- "Like sheep they are laid in the grave; death shall feed on them; and the upright shall have dominion over them in the morning" (v.14)
- "But God will redeem my soul from the power of the grave: for he shall receive me" (v.15)

**What the text does NOT say:**
- It does not say the soul is inherently immortal — on the contrary, it says no human can purchase endless life
- It does not say everyone goes to a place of conscious existence after death — it says they "are laid in the grave" and "death shall feed on them"

**Key observations:**
- The contrast between vv.9 and 15 is decisive: no human can purchase "living forever / not seeing corruption" (v.9), but God CAN redeem the soul from the grave (v.15). Immortality requires divine redemption — it is not a human inherent property.
- "Death shall feed on them" (v.14) — death consumes the wicked dead; they do not escape death. The grave "feeds on" them suggests genuine dissolution, not preservation in conscious torment.
- The psalmist's confidence is not in his own immortal soul but in God's power to redeem: "God will redeem my soul from the power of the grave" (v.15). The "power of the grave" is real and would prevail without God's intervention.

## 9. Christ's Victory Over Death

### Hebrews 2:14-15

**What the text says:**
- Christ "took part" in flesh and blood "that through death he might destroy him that had the power of death, that is, the devil"
- That he might "deliver them who through fear of death were all their lifetime subject to bondage"

**What the text does NOT say:**
- It does not say the devil's power over death is merely illusory or that humans were really immortal all along
- It does not say the fear of death is irrational because the soul is immortal

**Key observations:**
- The devil "had the power of death" (ton kratos ton thanatou) — death was a real power held by an adversary. This reinforces that death is not merely physical termination of the body but a ruling reality that holds humans in bondage.
- "All their lifetime subject to bondage" — the universality of this fear and bondage indicates that no human naturally escapes the reality of death. The bondage is universal; the deliverance comes only through Christ.
- Christ had to die TO destroy the one who had the power of death. If humans were already immortal, the logic of this passage would fail.

---

### 1 Corinthians 15:20-22, 26

**What the text says:**
- v.20: "Christ risen from the dead, and become the firstfruits of them that slept"
- v.21: "Since by man came death, by man came also the resurrection of the dead"
- v.22: "As in Adam all die, even so in Christ shall all be made alive"
- v.26: "The last enemy that shall be destroyed is death"

**What the text does NOT say:**
- It does not say the resurrection has already happened for believers in spirit or soul
- "Firstfruits" does not mean the entire harvest is complete; it means the first installment of a coming harvest

**Key observations:**
- "Firstfruits" — Christ's resurrection is the first installment of the general resurrection harvest. He is the model and guarantor, not the sole recipient.
- "In Adam all die" — the universality of death in Adam is mirrored by the universality of the potential for life in Christ. Both are corporate events.
- "The last enemy that shall be destroyed is death" (v.26) — death is still an active enemy at the time of writing. Its final destruction is future. This means the power of death is currently in operation; it has not yet been defeated in its experiential effect on humans (though Christ's resurrection has defeated it in principle).

## Patterns Identified

### Pattern 1: Immortality is exclusively divine in the present

In every NT passage where immortality/incorruption words are applied to the present tense:
- God alone "has" athanasia (1 Tim 6:16)
- God is aphthartos (Rom 1:23; 1 Tim 1:17)
- The Son has "life in himself" as a given divine property (John 5:26)

Not one NT text applies athanasia or aphtharsia as a present attribute of any human being. The vocabulary is consistent: the positive immortality words never describe the current human state.

### Pattern 2: Human beings are mortal and corruptible in the present

In every NT passage where thnetos (mortal) and phthartos (corruptible) appear:
- They uniformly describe the present human body/flesh
- Not one occurrence applies these terms to God or to the post-resurrection state

The vocabulary is consistent in the opposite direction: the negative mortality-words uniformly and exclusively describe the present human condition.

### Pattern 3: Immortality is a future gift received at the resurrection

The mechanism of receiving immortality is consistently: put on at the resurrection (1 Cor 15:53-54); raised incorruptible (1 Cor 15:52); body transformed at the last trump (1 Cor 15:51-52; Phil 3:21); mortal bodies quickened by the Spirit at the resurrection (Rom 8:11); cannot die "any more" only after obtaining the resurrection (Luk 20:36).

No text places the reception of immortality at death, at regeneration, or at birth.

### Pattern 4: Life is conditional on relationship with the Son

Across John's Gospel and the Johannine letters:
- "Whosoever believeth in him... have everlasting life" (John 3:16) — conditional on faith
- "He that hath the Son hath life; he that hath not the Son hath not life" (1 John 5:12) — conditional on having the Son
- "I give unto them eternal life" (John 10:28) — received from Christ, not inherent

The conditionality of eternal life is pervasive. Life is not a universal human endowment but a specific gift in the Son.

### Pattern 5: The OT framework — death as real, life as future promise

The OT consistently:
- Describes the dead as "sleeping in the dust" (Dan 12:2; cf. Job 7:21; Psa 22:15)
- Anticipates a future resurrection to everlasting life or everlasting contempt (Dan 12:2-3)
- Celebrates God's future victory over death as a real enemy (Isa 25:8; Hos 13:14)
- Never describes the dead as conscious in a positive intermediate state

The NT cites these OT texts to describe the resurrection event — the fulfillment of what the OT anticipated.

## Connections Between Passages

### The Tree of Life Bookend

Gen 2:9 (tree planted) → Gen 3:22-24 (tree blocked) → Rev 2:7 (tree promised to overcomers) → Rev 22:2,14 (tree restored in new creation)

This narrative arc spans the entire Bible. The tree of life is the consistent biblical symbol for immortality as an external divine provision, not an internal human property. Man's access to it was blocked after the fall; it is restored in the eschatological new creation. The pattern is: immortality was always external, conditional, and divinely mediated.

### The Death-Swallowed-Up Chain

Isa 25:8 ("He will swallow up death in victory") → 1 Cor 15:54 (quoted as fulfilled at the resurrection)
Hos 13:14 ("O death, I will be thy plagues") → 1 Cor 15:55 ("O death, where is thy sting?")
2 Tim 1:10 (Christ abolished death) → Heb 2:14-15 (Christ destroyed the power of death)

Paul's resurrection theology in 1 Cor 15 is built on OT texts. The death-as-enemy-to-be-destroyed theme runs from Isaiah and Hosea through Paul's resurrection chapter. Death is treated as a real power that requires divine action to defeat — not a mere appearance that immortal souls never genuinely face.

### The Mortal-Must-Put-On Chain

Rom 2:7 (humans seek immortality) → 1 Cor 15:53-54 (mortal must put on immortality at resurrection) → Luk 20:36 (after resurrection, cannot die anymore) → Rev 22:2,14 (access to tree of life in new creation)

The acquisition of immortality moves from seeking → receiving at resurrection → possessing forever. The sequence is coherent and consistent.

## Word Study Insights

### The Three Immortality Words: Distinct Emphasis, One Consistent Pattern

**G110 (athanasia):** Deathlessness — the absolute impossibility of dying. Occurs 3 times. God alone has it (1 Tim 6:16); mortals must put it on at resurrection (1 Cor 15:53-54).

**G861 (aphtharsia):** Incorruptibility — freedom from decay. Occurs 8 times. Humans seek it (Rom 2:7); Christ brought it to light (2 Tim 1:10); the body is raised in it (1 Cor 15:42-54).

**G862 (aphthartos):** Incorruptible (adjective). Occurs 7 times. Applied to God (Rom 1:23; 1 Tim 1:17), the resurrection body (1 Cor 15:52), the inheritance (1 Pet 1:4), the regenerating seed/word (1 Pet 1:23).

Not one occurrence of G110, G861, or G862 applies these concepts to the present human soul, spirit, or body as a current possession. The consistent pattern: these words belong to God and to the resurrection state.

### The Two Mortality Words: Universal Human Application

**G2349 (thnetos):** Mortal — subject to death. Occurs 6 times. In every occurrence: present human body/flesh.

**G5349 (phthartos):** Corruptible — subject to decay. Occurs 6 times. In every occurrence: present human body/man (including the explicit God-vs-man contrast in Rom 1:23).

### No Hebrew Equivalent for Athanasia

The OT has no word equivalent to athanasia (deathlessness). The Hebrew vocabulary handles the concept through: chay le-olam (live forever, Gen 3:22 — blocked); chayyei olam (everlasting life, Dan 12:2 — future promise); the tree of life narrative. The OT approach is: immortality is a future divine gift, not a present human property. The NT's athanasia sharpens the concept with Greek precision but does not change the substance.

## Difficult Passages

### 1 John 5:12 and Present Tense Possession

The verse says "he that hath the Son hath life" — present tense. This seems to indicate that believers presently possess eternal life. This is not a "difficult" passage for the conditionalist position — it is consistent with conditional immortality as follows: the believer presently has eternal life as a covenantal status, a real present possession in Christ, not yet fully manifest in body-transformation. The question is whether "having eternal life" in a present covenantal sense means the soul is currently in an immortal state.

The context of 1 John 5:11 shows that "this life is in his Son" — the life is located in the Son. Having the Son = having the life that is in him. This is relational possession, not soul-state description.

### Luke 20:38 — "All live unto him"

Jesus says "he is not a God of the dead, but of the living: for all live unto him." This could be read as meaning all the patriarchs are currently alive unto God in a conscious state. However:

1. The grammatical context (v.37-38) is Jesus's argument from the covenant — God is called the God of Abraham, Isaac, and Jacob even after their physical deaths. The argument is about God's covenantal faithfulness and resurrection commitment, not about the patriarchs' current conscious state.
2. "All live unto him" — the dative "unto him" (autos) suggests "in relation to/for him," not "before him in conscious awareness." God's relationship with the covenant people is not terminated by their death.
3. This passage is addressed by the resurrection context (v.35-36) — the "life" in view is resurrection life, not intermediate-state consciousness.
4. This will receive fuller treatment in dedicated studies on specific intermediate-state passages.

### Matthew 10:28 — "Not able to kill the soul"

As noted above, this verse says men cannot kill the soul but God can destroy both. The phrase "cannot kill the soul" has been used to argue for the soul's indestructibility. However:

- The verse explicitly provides the counter-claim: God CAN destroy both soul and body in gehenna. If the soul were indestructible by nature, the verse would require a strong qualifier ("God can apparently-destroy" or "God can put into a state that feels like destruction"). The text provides no such qualifier.
- The distinction is between human power (insufficient to kill the soul) and divine power (sufficient to destroy both). The soul's resistance to human killing does not constitute inherent indestructibility.
