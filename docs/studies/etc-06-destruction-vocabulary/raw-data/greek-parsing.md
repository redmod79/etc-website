**Key grammatical note on ἀπόληται:**
- Form: 2nd Aorist Middle Subjunctive, 3rd Person Singular
- The aorist aspect typically expresses a discrete, complete event (not ongoing process)
- Middle voice: reflexive nuance — "perish" as something that happens to/in the subject
- Subjunctive mood used because of the ἵνα (purpose/result clause): "in order that... he should not perish"
- The purpose clause creates a binary: either perish (apollumi — complete destruction) OR have eternal life (zoe aionios)
- The antithesis is structural: NOT perish // BUT have life — two completely different outcomes

## 2 Thessalonians 1:9 — olethron aionion

**Greek Text:** οἵτινες δίκην τίσουσιν ὄλεθρον αἰώνιον ἀπὸ προσώπου τοῦ Κυρίου καὶ ἀπὸ τῆς δόξης τῆς ἰσχύος αὐτοῦ,

**Key grammatical notes:**
- "ὄλεθρον αἰώνιον" (olethron aionion) = "everlasting/age-lasting destruction"
- olethros (G3639) = ruin, destruction — never "torment" in any NT occurrence
- The phrase "ἀπὸ προσώπου τοῦ Κυρίου" (from the face/presence of the Lord) is key to interpretation
  - "apo" (from, away from) can mean: (a) sent/caused by the Lord, or (b) banished from the Lord's presence
  - Both readings are grammatically possible; context determines
- "aionion" (G166/G165) attached to olethros — not to basanismos or basanizo
  - The eternality/permanence qualifier attaches to the destruction term, not the torment term
  - This argues that what is "eternal/age-lasting" is the destruction itself (or its effects), not ongoing suffering

## Matthew 25:46 — kolasin aionion vs. zoen aionion

**Greek Text:** καὶ ἀπελεύσονται οὗτοι εἰς κόλασιν αἰώνιον, οἱ δὲ δίκαιοι εἰς ζωὴν αἰώνιον.

**Key grammatical notes:**
- The same adjective (αἰώνιον/aionion) modifies both κόλασιν (punishment) and ζωὴν (life)
- The parallel structure requires that αἰώνιος apply in the same sense to both outcomes
- IF aionios means "conscious experience forever" for the wicked, then zoen aionion = "conscious experience forever" for the righteous (which is also what Christians believe)
- IF aionios means "permanent/irreversible in its effects" for the wicked, then zoen aionion = "permanent/irreversible life" for the righteous (also consistent with Christian doctrine)
- kolasis (G2851): penal infliction — from kolazo = to prune, curtail, chastise; involves punitive purpose
- Note: The vocabulary of this verse is "punishment" (kolasis), NOT "destruction" (olethros/apollumi); this verse does not use the primary destruction vocabulary — it uses punishment vocabulary

**Key grammatical notes:**
- "ἀπολέσαι" = Aorist Active Infinitive of apollumi (G622)
- Aorist: treats the destruction as a single, complete event
- Active: God is the agent — he destroys (causative/active role)
- Object: BOTH "ψυχὴν" (soul) AND "σῶμα" (body) — explicitly both
- Location: "ἐν γεέννῃ" (in gehenna) — the place of destruction is gehenna

  God can destroy (apollumi) BOTH
- The word for human killing is apokteinō (to kill); the word for God's action is apollumi (to destroy fully)
  This distinction suggests God's action is more thorough/complete than mere killing

**Greek Text:** ὧν τὸ τέλος ἀπώλεια, ὧν ὁ θεὸς ἡ κοιλία καὶ ἡ δόξα ἐν τῇ αἰσχύνῃ αὐτῶν, οἱ τὰ ἐπίγεια φρονοῦντες.

**Key grammatical note:**
- "τέλος ἀπώλεια" = "the end is destruction"
- telos = end, goal, outcome, conclusion
- apoleia = destruction, perdition — as the final outcome/telos
- Predicate nominative (no verb stated, understood as "is")
- The enemies of the cross have destruction as their telos — their final state/outcome
