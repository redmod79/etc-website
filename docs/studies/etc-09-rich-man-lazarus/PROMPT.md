# Bible Study: The Parable of the Rich Man and Lazarus (Luke 16:19-31)

## Study ID
etc5-09-rich-man-lazarus

---

## Question

What is the literary genre of Luke 16:19-31 — is it a parable or a literal historical account of the afterlife? What does the passage say directly? What do the specific details mean in their first-century Jewish context? Does this passage prove eternal conscious torment in hades, or a conscious intermediate state between death and resurrection, or neither? How should IB006 (Luke 16 as literal hades description) be classified after a full dedicated investigation?

---

## Central Interpretive Issue

This passage has been classified as **IB006** (I-B Unresolved) since etc5-03-biblical-death (first introduced) and confirmed Unresolved in etc5-04, etc5-05, and through etc5-08. The genre determination — parable or literal — is the crux of the entire interpretive question. This study's primary task is to gather ALL the evidence and attempt a SIS resolution of IB006.

**The two positions:**

- **ECT / Conscious Intermediate State position:** Luke 16:19-31 is a literal account of the actual afterlife, not a parable. Jesus would not teach falsehood even in a parable, but the named characters (Abraham, Lazarus), the specific geographic details (Abraham's bosom, hades, the great gulf), and the conscious dialogue confirm this describes a real intermediate state where the wicked suffer torment and the righteous enjoy comfort immediately after death.

- **Conditionalist / Parabolic position:** Luke 16:19-31 is a parable — a literary device using familiar imagery from Jewish intertestamental sources (1 Enoch, Apocalypse of Moses) to make a theological point. The stated purpose of the passage is v.29-31 (the sufficiency of Moses and the prophets). The physical impossibilities (fingers dipping in water cooling tongues), the reversal-of-fortunes structure (a hallmark of Lukan parables), and the conflict with plain state-of-the-dead texts (Ecc 9:5; Psa 146:4; Job 14:12) indicate this is not a doctrinal map of the intermediate state.

### Focus Areas (Tool-Discovered)

1. **Genre determination — is the word parabolē present?**
   - Tool finding: G3850 parabolē does NOT appear in Luke 16:19-31
   - But it also does NOT appear in Luke 15:11-32 (Prodigal Son) — which everyone accepts as a parable
   - Luke 15:3 uses parabolē to introduce the lost sheep PERICOPE, which may cover all three parables in Luke 15
   - The Pharisee and Tax Collector (Luk 18:9-14) and the Unjust Judge (Luk 18:1-8) are introduced with parabolē
   - Question: Does the absence of parabolē in Luke 16 prove it is not a parable, or is this a genre feature of certain Lukan parables?

2. **Named characters — does Lazarus's naming prove it is literal?**
   - Lazarus is the only named character in any parable of Jesus (ECT argument: therefore it is not a parable)
   - The rich man has NO name in the passage itself (some traditions call him "Dives" from the Vulgate, but the Greek has no name)
   - The "father" in the Prodigal Son is also unnamed; the prodigal himself is unnamed
   - Abraham is named but Abraham appears in parables and literal contexts

3. **The reversal-of-fortunes structure — a Lukan parabolic signature**
   - The passage explicitly says in v.25: "thou in thy lifetime receivedst thy good things, and likewise Lazarus evil things: but now he is comforted, and thou art tormented"
   - This exact reversal-of-fortunes structure appears throughout Luke's Gospel (Luk 1:52-53; 6:20-26; 14:11)
   - Abraham's bosom as the "good place" for the poor man who suffered in life — reversal pattern
   - The Beatitudes and Woes in Luke 6:20-26 use the identical pattern

4. **The passage's theological purpose (v.29-31) — Moses and the prophets**
   - Cross-testament parallel search confirms: Luk 16:31 parallels (Luk 16:29, 0.507), Act 3:22 (0.458), Luk 20:37 (0.437), Luk 24:44 (0.400) — all about Moses and the prophets as the sufficient revelation
   - The theological POINT of the passage is: "they have Moses and the prophets; let them hear them" (v.29) and "If they hear not Moses and the prophets, neither will they be persuaded, though one rose from the dead" (v.31)
   - This is the STATED PURPOSE of the narrative — directed at the Pharisees (v.14: "Pharisees... derided him")
   - Even if the imagery is borrowed from intertestamental Jewish literature, the theological point is the sufficiency of existing revelation

5. **The context: covetous Pharisees (Luke 16:14-15)**
   - v.14: "And the Pharisees also, who were covetous, heard all these things: and they derided him"
   - v.15: "Ye are they which justify yourselves before men; but God knoweth your hearts: for that which is highly esteemed among men is abomination in the sight of God"
   - The rich man in the parable is the TYPE of the covetous Pharisee who values wealth and external honor; Lazarus is the type of the poor sinner whom the Pharisees despise
   - The narrative is Jesus's direct response to the deriding Pharisees

6. **The great gulf (chasma, G5490) — hapax legomenon**
   - G5490 chasma occurs ONLY in Luke 16:26 in the entire NT
   - The gulf is "fixed" (esteEriktai) — a perfect passive indicative, a permanent completed state
   - The gulf prevents movement between the two regions
   - This is a feature of Jewish intertestamental literature on the divided afterlife (1 Enoch 22; Test. Abraham; Apocalypse of Zephaniah)

7. **Abraham's bosom (kolpos, G2859) as a Jewish idiom**
   - G2859 kolpos appears in Luk 16:22,23; also Jhn 1:18 ("only begotten Son, which is in the bosom of the Father") and Jhn 13:23 (John "leaning on Jesus' bosom" at the Last Supper)
   - "Abraham's bosom" was a recognized Jewish idiom for the place of honor at the messianic banquet (compare Mat 8:11: "many shall come from the east and west, and shall sit down with Abraham, and Isaac, and Jacob, in the kingdom of heaven")
   - The idiom does not require a literal anatomical bosom — it is a figure of fellowship and honor

8. **Hades (G86) and its function — against using Luke 16 as a doctrinal map**
   - N026 (established in etc5-04): Hades is temporary; it receives → holds → delivers up at resurrection → is destroyed (Rev 20:13-14)
   - E130/E131 (Acts 2:29,31): David was dead and buried in hades as of Pentecost; his soul was in hades until the resurrection
   - If the rich man in Luk 16 is in pre-resurrection hades, this is consistent with N026 (hades as temporary holding)
   - But if this is a description of the final fate, it is NOT — hades is not the lake of fire (Rev 20:14 distinguishes them)
   - The research agent must clarify: does Luke 16 describe hades (pre-resurrection) or a final state?

9. **The physical impossibilities — parabolic indicators?**
   - A literal reading requires: disembodied souls have eyes (v.23), fingers (v.24), tongues (v.24), and can experience thirst
   - The rich man's five brothers are still alive on earth (v.28) — placing the narrative in the pre-resurrection period
   - The conversation across the gulf parallels the narrative features of fictional dialogues in Jewish literature
   - ECT response: these are accommodations to human understanding, not necessarily literal; the spiritual reality they describe is real

10. **Luke 16:16-18 bridge verses — the Law/Prophets connection**
    - v.16: "The law and the prophets were until John"
    - v.17: "It is easier for heaven and earth to pass, than one tittle of the law to fail"
    - v.18: A teaching on adultery
    - v.19: "There was a certain rich man..." — the parable begins immediately after these teachings
    - The three bridge sayings and the parable are all addressed to the Pharisees (v.14-15 context)
    - v.29-31 circles back to "Moses and the prophets" — the parable is bracketed by Law/Prophets references

11. **Paradise (G3857, paradeisos) — is Luke 23:43 equivalent to Abraham's bosom?**
    - G3857 appears only 3 times in the NT (Luk 23:43; 2Co 12:4; Rev 2:7)
    - Abraham's bosom is NOT called paradise in Luke 16 — the word paradise does not appear in the passage
    - ECT position: Abraham's bosom = paradise = the conscious blessed intermediate state
    - Conditionalist position: the connection between "Abraham's bosom" and "paradise" is an assumption, not an explicit equation in the text
    - 2Co 12:4 places paradise in "the third heaven" — not necessarily equivalent to the pre-resurrection hades of Luke 16

12. **Semantic comparison: basanizō (G928) in Luke 16 vs. the NT**
    - G928 basanizō (12 NT occurrences): Mat 8:6 (servant sick, tormented); Mat 8:29 (demons: "art thou come to torment us before the time?"); Mat 14:24 (ship "tossed with waves"); Mk 5:7 (demon: "torment me not"); Mk 6:48 (disciples "toiling in rowing"); Luk 8:28 (demon: "torment me not"); 2Pe 2:8 (Lot "vexed" by Sodom's evil); Rev 9:5; 11:10; 12:2; 14:10; 20:10
    - Note: Mk 6:48 uses basanizō for physical toiling (disciples rowing) — not a supernatural torment
    - Note: Mat 8:29 — the demon says "art thou come to torment us BEFORE THE TIME?" — implying that permanent torment is a future eschatological event, not a current intermediate-state reality
